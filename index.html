Never have humans seen deeper into space than with the technology of today. Yet, fewer and fewer people are able to see the universe with their own eyes. Light pollution from developed areas can drown out the stars.

Light pollution affects 80% of the world’s population and 99% of the US population. This is not only a problem for astronomers, but light pollution wastes energy and can affect sleep, road safety and ecosystems.

Have you ever been out at night and seen a glow in the distance? More than likely, that glow arose from the lights of a town or city. According to the International Dark Sky Association, “much outdoor lighting used at night is inefficient, overly bright, poorly targeted, improperly shielded, and, in many cases, completely unnecessary.” This interferes with our view of the stars, but also represents wasted energy.

Sources such as exterior and interior lighting, advertising and streetlights can all contribute to light pollution. However, different sorts of lighting create different sorts of pollution.

    Skyglow

    Glare

    Trespass

    Clutter

    Uplight

The International Darksky Association recommends how people and their communities can reduce light pollution. Lights should be:

  • Only be on when needed
  • Only light the area that needs it
  • Be no brighter than necessary
  • Minimize blue light emissions
  • Be fully shielded (pointing downward)

In addition to recommendations for how you improve your lighting at home, the IDA provides a guide for municipalities to implement changes in their community.
